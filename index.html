Key Generation is the first crucial step in cryptography. In this screenshot, we delve into the process of generating cryptographic keys. It begins with the calculation of N, which is the product of two large prime numbers, P and Q. These prime numbers are chosen randomly to enhance security. Next, we determine the public key, denoted as 'e,' and the private key, denoted as 'd.' The criteria for selecting these keys are outlined in the screenshot. To compute 'e,' we employ Euler's totient function, which involves understanding the concept of Phi of N (Ï•(N)), calculated as (P-1) * (Q-1). Sending secure messages involves not only encrypting the message but also ensuring that only the intended recipient can decrypt and read it. Key distribution plays a crucial role in achieving this goal. When person A wants to send a confidential message to person B without the risk of interception by eavesdroppers, they rely on key distribution. Person A needs to know person B's public key, denoted as e, and the modulus N. These values, which are necessary for encryption, can be openly distributed and are accessible to anyone, including potential eavesdroppers. However, for person B to decrypt the message and access its contents, they require their private key d, in addition to the modulus N. Unlike the public key, which can be openly shared, the private key d is kept confidential and known only to person B. This ensures that only person B, the intended recipient, possesses the means to decipher the encrypted message, thus preserving the confidentiality of the communication.

The process of encrypting a message ensures that it remains confidential during transmission. When person A sends a message to person B, it begins in a format known as unpadded plaintext M. However, before sending the message, A and B agree upon a padding scheme. This scheme allows A to transform M into m, a padded plaintext usually represented in numerical form. Once m is prepared, A employs a mathematical expression, as depicted in the screenshot, to compute the ciphertext c. This expression involves modular exponentiation and ensures that the message is securely encoded. Subsequently, A transmits c to B, who will then proceed to decrypt it using their private key d and the modulus N.

Upon receiving the ciphertext c from person A, person B initiates the decryption process. The first step involves unpacking m from c. Once m is extracted, B utilizes the agreed-upon padding scheme to recover the original plaintext message M. This process allows B to decipher the encrypted message securely sent by A. Thus, through the combined efforts of encryption and decryption, A successfully transmitted a confidential message to B, who then successfully decrypted it, ensuring the confidentiality and integrity of the communication.
